About the show

The Office (US) is a mockumentary sitcom, Created by Greg Daniels, that aired on NBC from March 24, 2005 to May 16, 2013. Adapted from the British series of the same name, the show follows the day-to-day lives of the employees at the Dunder Mifflin Paper Company branch in Scranton, Pennsylvania, filmed in faux-documentary style.

Across 9 seasons and 186 episodes, the ensemble is anchored by regional manager Michael Scott (Steve Carell, S1–7), salesman Jim Halpert, receptionist (later artist/sales rep) Pam Beesly, and eccentric beet-farming salesman Dwight Schrute. Around them rotates a deep cast of accountants, salespeople, warehouse staff, and corporate types — Andy, Angela, Kevin, Oscar, Stanley, Phyllis, Kelly, Ryan, Toby, Erin, Darryl and many more.

Design notes & justification (click to expand)

Tasks supported

  1. Show overview — the header and stats panel give the network, run dates, season/episode counts, genre, and a short prose intro to the central characters.
  2. Importance overall & per-season — the bar chart ranks characters by either lines, words, or episodes appeared. The season dropdown (and clicking a season label in the heatmap) re-computes the ranking for just that season, so a viewer can compare e.g. Andy in S8 vs. the show as a whole.
  3. Per-episode appearances for major characters — the heatmap shows one row per major character and one column per episode, color-encoded by lines spoken in that episode. Empty (gray) cells = the character didn’t speak that episode. Season dividers segment the timeline.
  4. Character co-occurrence — the chord diagram shows which characters share scenes most often. Each arc is a character, and each chord connecting two arcs is sized by how many scenes they both spoke in. The season dropdown re-builds the diagram for just that season, so you can see e.g. how Michael’s scene partners shift from S1 to S7, or who fills the gap in S8–9. The top-10 pairs bar chart gives the exact counts.

Visual encoding choices

  • Horizontal bar chart for ranked importance: bar length is the most accurate visual channel for quantitative comparison, and horizontal bars give long character names room to breathe.
  • Heatmap for character × episode: with ~25 characters × 186 episodes (~4,650 cells), a heatmap is the most space-efficient way to expose appearance patterns — you can see seasons where a character vanishes (Michael after S7), recurring guests, and standout episodes (high-line bursts) at a glance.
  • Square-root color scale on the heatmap so a few high-line "Michael episodes" don’t crush the rest of the palette into invisibility.
  • Brushing & linking: selecting a character in the bar chart highlights its row in the heatmap; choosing a season in the dropdown both re-ranks the bar chart and dims non-matching episodes in the heatmap. This keeps the two views consistent and supports answering "who matters in S6?" and "where in S6 did Andy actually show up?" together.
  • Tooltips for details-on-demand instead of cluttering the chart with labels.
  • Wikipedia link next to each bar (the small w) for the optional "link out" requirement.
  • Chord diagram for co-occurrence: character interactions form a weighted network (who shares scenes with whom), and a chord diagram is a natural fit, since arc length encodes how connected a character is overall, and chord thickness encodes the strength of a specific pairing. Hovering an arc dims all unrelated chords so you can isolate one character’s connections without losing context.
  • Top pairs bar chart alongside the chord diagram: chords are good for seeing the overall shape of the network but hard to rank precisely. For example, is the Michael to Jim chord thicker than Michael toDwight? The bar chart answers that a little more directly.

Who is included

The dataset has hundreds of named speakers, most of whom appear once. To keep the dashboard about meaningful characters we include only speakers with ≥ 100 total lines AND appearing in ≥ 5 episodes, then take the top characters by total lines (capped at 25 to keep the heatmap readable). Compound speakers like “Jim & Pam” and group labels (“Everyone”, “All”) are excluded; small typo variants (“Michael:”, “DeAngelo”) are normalized to their canonical form. This excludes one-line guest characters but keeps every recurring character with a meaningful presence in at least a few episodes.

Theme

The cream/mustard/dark-brown palette and Bebas Neue title evoke the show’s opening titles and Dunder Mifflin’s drab office aesthetic.

Why option 2 (co-occurrence) for Level 3

Level 2 already covers what characters say, so Level 3 picks up option 2 — who speaks with whom. The Office is an ensemble show and character pairings drive most of the comedy (Jim to Dwight, Michael to Toby, Jim to Pam), so a co-occurrence view felt more revealing than duplicating text analysis. The transcript data includes scene numbers per episode, which gives a clean way to infer who appears together without guessing from adjacent lines.
